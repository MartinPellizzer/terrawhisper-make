IA (Information Architecture)

PASE 1. semantic entity extraction
    - define core entity (herbal medicine)
    - define related entities
    - define attributes

PASE 2. topical map (hierarchy)
    - level 1. root (topical pillar)
        - subject: intro herbal medicine (theory, history, ...)

    - level 2. semantic clusters (thematic silos)
        Divide the subject into mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive (MECE) clusters:
        Cluster A: Botanical Identification 
            (Taxonomy, Morphology, Seasonality).
        Cluster B: Phytochemistry 
            (Alkaloids, Flavonoids, Terpenes, Tannins).
        Cluster C: Materia Medica 
            (Specific herbs: e.g., Echinacea, Ashwagandha).
        Cluster D: Preparation Methods 
            (Tinctures, Infusions, Decoctions, Salves).
        Cluster E: Safety & Toxicology 
            (Drug interactions, Side effects).

    - level 3. granular nodes (long-tail/specifics)
        Ashwagandha -> 
            Ashwagandha for Sleep 
            Ashwagandha dosage in Tincture vs. Powder.


PASE 3. Establishing Semantic Connectivity (The Internal Linking)
In this framework, links are not just for SEO; they are **Semantic Bridges**. You must link nodes based on their relationship in the real world.

*   **Thematic Links:** Link "Valerian" to "Sedative Herbs."
*   **Functional Links:** Link "Alkaloids" to "Echinacea" (explaining *why* the chemical causes the effect).
*   **Contextual Links:** Link "Decoctions" to "Hardy Roots" (explaining *how* to prepare specific plant parts).

**The Rule:** Every time you mention a concept in an article that has its own dedicated page, you **must** link to it. This builds a "Web of Context."

### 4. Phase Four: Content Coverage & Semantic Density
GÜBÜR’s methodology requires you to satisfy the **Search Intent** through **Information Foraging**. For "Learning Herbal Medicine," your content must answer the "How, Why, and What" to cover the semantic space.

**For every "Herb" page, you must cover these Semantic Properties to achieve Topical Authority:**
1.  **Taxonomy:** Scientific name, Family.
2.  **Morphology:** What does the plant look like?
3.  **Phytochemistry:** What are the active compounds?
4.  **Pharmacology:** How does it work in the human body?
5.  **Therapeutic Use:** What conditions does it treat?
6.  **Preparation:** How is it extracted?
7.  **Safety:** Who should avoid it?

*If you miss "Phytochemistry" on a herb page, your semantic coverage is incomplete, and your authority is capped.*

---

### 5. Phase Five: Implementation Strategy (The Workflow)

To avoid "Content Decay" and "Information Gaps," follow this order:

1.  **Start with the "Definition" and "Theory" nodes:** Build the foundation so the search engine understands your "Context."
2.  **Build the "Categorical" nodes:** Define your clusters (e.g., "Herbs for Digestion").
3.  **Build the "Entity" nodes:** Create the individual herb pages.
4.  **Connect upwards and downwards:** Use a "Hub and Spoke" model.

#### Example Content Map for a single node:
*   **Article 1 (Hub):** The Ultimate Guide to Herbal Tinctures.
*   **Article 2 (Spoke):** How to choose an alcohol percentage for tinctures.
*   **Article 3 (Spoke):** Using Glycerin as a solvent for children's tinctures.
*   **Article 4 (Deep Spoke):** The chemical stability of Valerian in a 40% ABV tincture.

### Summary Checklist for your "Learning Herbal Medicine" Site:
*   [ ] **Does every page have a clear Entity?** (e.g., not "How to use it," but "How to use *Peppermint*")

*   [ ] **Is there a logical hierarchy?** (General $\rightarrow$ Specific)
*   [ ] **Is there "Semantic Completeness"?** (Do you cover chemistry, safety, and usage for every herb?)
*   [ ] **Are links meaningful?** (Do links explain the relationship between two concepts?)
